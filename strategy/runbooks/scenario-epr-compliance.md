# Runbook: EPR Compliance Engagement

> **Scenario**: A company produces or sells packaging — or products that become packaging — and needs to navigate the rapidly expanding landscape of Extended Producer Responsibility laws. CA SB 54 is in effect. OR HB 2592 is in effect. ME, CO, MN, and MD have enacted their own programs. The EU PPWR is advancing. This engagement maps the full EPR exposure, registers with the right Producer Responsibility Organizations (PROs), calculates fees, designs a compliance roadmap, and stress-tests packaging claims before they reach the public.

---

## When to Use This Runbook

- Company sells products with packaging into US states with EPR laws (CA, OR, ME, CO, MN, MD)
- Company sells into EU markets subject to EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) or national EPR programs
- Company is receiving EPR compliance notices or fee invoices and needs to understand exposure
- Company is redesigning packaging and wants to understand EPR implications (eco-modulation)
- Company wants to make packaging sustainability claims and needs greenwashing clearance

## Phase Breakdown

### Phase 1 (Weeks 1–2): Regulatory Exposure Mapping

**EPR Specialist**:

**US EPR landscape assessment**:
- **California SB 54 (Plastic Pollution Prevention and Packaging Producer Responsibility Act)**:
  - Covered producers: any entity that sells, offers for sale, or imports into CA a product with packaging
  - Compliance: register with CalRecycle-approved PRO; pay fees based on plastic tonnage sold into CA
  - Requirements by 2030: 30% reduction in plastic packaging; 65% recycled content; all plastic packaging recyclable, compostable, or reusable
  - Eco-modulation: fees adjusted based on recyclability, recycled content, and reuse attributes

- **Oregon HB 2592 (Plastic Pollution and Recycling Modernization Act)**:
  - Covered producers: all packaging supplied to Oregon consumers
  - PRO registration required by July 2025 (first year)
  - Fees based on material type and weight; eco-modulated for recyclability and recycled content
  - Labeling: How2Recycle label or equivalent required for recycling claims

- **Maine LD 1541**: First US statewide EPR program enacted; comprehensive packaging registration
- **Colorado SB 21-176**: EPR program with advisory board; regulations under development
- **Minnesota SF 3911**: EPR legislation with packaging redesign requirements
- **Maryland**: EPR law enacted; program development ongoing

**EU EPR landscape assessment**:
- **EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR)**: proposed revision to PPWR Directive; will require all packaging to be recyclable by 2030 (end market recycling rates); mandatory recycled content targets by packaging type; restrictions on single-use plastic packaging for specific applications
- **National EPR programs**: Germany (VerpackG/LUCID), France (CITEO), Italy (CONAI), Spain (ECOEMBES) — existing programs with fee structures and reporting requirements
- **EU Taxonomy alignment**: packaging recyclability relevant to Substantial Contribution to E5 (circular economy)

**Output**: Regulatory exposure map — by jurisdiction, by program, by deadline, by estimated fee exposure

### Phase 2 (Weeks 2–4): Packaging Data Collection

**EPR Specialist + Scope 3 Analyst**:

1. Identify all packaging types: primary, secondary, tertiary (transport) by material (plastic, paper, glass, metal, multi-material)
2. Quantify plastic tonnage by type (PET, HDPE, LDPE, PP, PS, PVC, other) sold into each EPR-applicable jurisdiction
3. Identify current recyclability status per material type: How2Recycle end-market study data; access to facilities (ATF) assessment
4. Identify current recycled content % per packaging type: supply chain data; certifications
5. Identify packaging that is reusable vs. single-use

### Phase 3 (Weeks 3–6): Fee Calculation and PRO Registration

**EPR Specialist**:

**California SB 54 fee calculation**:
- Calculate plastic tonnage by type sold into CA per year
- Apply PRO fee schedule (once published by approved PRO)
- Eco-modulation adjustments: recyclability credit; recycled content credit; reusable packaging credit
- Estimate annual fee exposure: Year 1, Year 3, Year 5

**Oregon fee calculation**:
- Calculate total packaging weight by material sold into OR
- Apply fee schedule per material type (base fee × eco-modulation factor)
- Labeling compliance: How2Recycle required on packaging sold in OR

### Phase 4 (Weeks 5–8): Compliance Roadmap and Packaging Redesign

**EPR Specialist + Circular Economy Strategist**:

1. **Recyclability improvement opportunities**: identify packaging types currently failing recyclability requirements; engineering options (material switch, coating removal, additive elimination, material consolidation)
2. **Recycled content increase pathway**: identify rPCR/rPIR supply options; cost premium; supply security; GRS certification pathway
3. **Reusable packaging assessment**: which packaging types could transition to reuse models? Cost-benefit; consumer acceptance; logistics requirements
4. **Eco-modulation optimization**: what changes would reduce EPR fees while improving sustainability performance?
5. **Design for circularity**: apply How2Recycle DRC (Demand for Recyclability Certification) criteria; Ellen MacArthur Foundation New Plastics Economy design principles
6. **Timeline**: 2025 → 2027 → 2030 milestones aligned with SB 54, OR, and EU PPWR requirements

**Deliverable**: Packaging redesign roadmap; eco-modulation optimization analysis; fee trajectory under current state vs. redesigned packaging

### Phase 5 (Weeks 4–6 + Final): Greenwashing Audit of Packaging Claims

**Greenwashing Auditor**:

Packaging sustainability claims are among the highest-risk claims for FTC enforcement, EU regulatory action, and consumer protection lawsuits. Every claim on packaging must pass this audit before printing.

**High-risk claims to audit**:

| Claim | Risk | Standard | Audit Questions |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Recyclable" | HIGH | FTC §260.12; ISO 14021 | Is it recyclable in communities where sold? Access to facilities? What % of US population? How2Recycle tier? |
| "Made with recycled content" | MEDIUM | FTC §260.13; ISO 14021 | Pre-consumer or post-consumer? What %? Chain of custody cert? |
| "Compostable" | HIGH | FTC §260.8; ISO 14021 | Industrial or home compostable? Infrastructure availability? Conditions disclosed? |
| "Biodegradable" | HIGH | FTC §260.7; EU Empowering Consumers Dir. | Timeframe? Environment? EU bans unqualified use |
| "Made from ocean plastic" | HIGH | FTC; regulatory scrutiny | How is "ocean plastic" defined? Chain of custody? % of content? |
| "Eco-friendly packaging" | CRITICAL | FTC §260.4; EU Empowering Consumers Dir. | Unqualified environmental claim — fails FTC general principle; EU Empowering Consumers bans vague claims |
| "Sustainable packaging" | CRITICAL | FTC §260.4 | Unqualified — same as above |
| "100% recycled" | HIGH | FTC §260.13 | Pre- or post-consumer? Both? % verified? |

**"Recyclable" claim scrutiny** (highest risk):
- FTC requires recyclable claim to be true for a "substantial majority" of consumers — currently interpreted as ≥60% of US population having access to recycling programs for the material
- How2Recycle tier must be disclosed: "Widely Recycled" vs. "Check Locally" vs. "Store Drop-Off" — these are not all equal
- Using "Recyclable" label on packaging that is only store drop-off recyclable without disclosure = FTC violation risk
- EU PPWR: recyclability claims must align with actual recyclability at end-market rates ≥70%

**Greenwashing audit output format**:

```markdown
## Packaging Claim Audit — [Product/Brand] — [Date]

| Claim | Location | Claim Type | Evidence | Standard | Risk | Verdict | Required Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| "Widely Recyclable" | Primary label (HDPE bottle) | Recyclability | How2Recycle cert — Widely Recycled (HDPE curbside) | FTC §260.12; How2Recycle | LOW | PASS | None |
| "100% Recyclable Packaging" | Website product page | Recyclability (corporate claim) | HDPE primary = Widely Recyclable; LDPE shrink = Store Drop-Off only | FTC §260.12 | HIGH | FAIL — not all packaging is curbside recyclable | Replace with: "Primary packaging Widely Recyclable (HDPE); secondary packaging store drop-off recyclable (LDPE)" |
| "Sustainable Packaging" | Marketing materials | Unqualified env claim | No specific substantiation | FTC §260.4; EU ECD | CRITICAL | FAIL | Remove. Replace with specific substantiated attribute |
| "Made with 30% recycled content" | Secondary packaging | Recycled content | GRS certificate for 30% rPCR LDPE | FTC §260.13; ISO 14021 | LOW | PASS — with pre/post consumer disclosure | Add: "(30% post-consumer recycled)" |
```

## Phase 6 (Weeks 8–10): Client Deliverables

**Client Deliverable Generator**:

For Legal / Compliance:
- EPR compliance status report: which programs; registration status; fee exposure; key deadlines
- Regulatory risk memo: non-compliance consequences; fee exposure range; litigation risk from packaging claims
- Packaging claim approved language list: what can be said; what cannot; FTC/EU/UK standards applied

For Operations / Supply Chain / Packaging Engineering:
- Packaging redesign roadmap: actionable changes by packaging type; eco-modulation impact; cost analysis
- How2Recycle program integration guide: how to apply for and maintain How2Recycle labels
- PRO registration and reporting guide: how to comply with each program's ongoing reporting requirements

For Marketing / Communications:
- Approved claims library: pre-cleared language for each packaging type with substantiation backup
- "What not to say" list: high-risk claims with specific reasons
- FAQ: responses to likely consumer and press questions about packaging sustainability

For CFO:
- EPR fee exposure: current year; 3-year projection; 2030 projection under current state vs. redesign
- Cost-benefit: packaging redesign investment vs. fee reduction; eco-modulation optimization value
